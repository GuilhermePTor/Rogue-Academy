MODULE_01 // THEORETICAL_MINIMUM

Statistical Mechanics

📗
An Introduction to Thermal Physics
Daniel V. Schroeder
▶ Susskind — Theoretical Minimum ↗
Statistical mechanics is where ML borrows most heavily from physics. The Boltzmann distribution is the mathematical ancestor of softmax. Partition functions appear throughout probabilistic modelling. Free energy minimization is what variational inference is actually doing. The entropy studied here is the same entropy that defines information content, uncertainty in predictions, and regularization in neural networks. After this module, gradient descent starts to look like a system seeking its lowest energy state — because formally, it is.

Problems 2.1 – 2.3 ML LINK
Counting arrangements of coins and spins. Build the combinatorial intuition that Ω → ln Ω → entropy. This is the exact same operation as computing the entropy of a probability distribution in information theory — and the foundation of cross-entropy loss.

Shannon entropy and thermodynamic entropy are the same quantity up to the constant k₂. Every time you minimise cross-entropy loss in a neural network you are minimising a thermodynamic quantity. Understanding why entropy measures uncertainty — not just that it does — is what makes KL divergence and the information bottleneck principle legible later.

The Boltzmann distribution is the softmax function. P(state i) = e−βEᵢ/Z is identical in form to softmax(logits/T). The "temperature" T in language model sampling is the literal thermodynamic temperature. Energy-based models, Gibbs sampling, simulated annealing, and the attention mechanism all trace back to this derivation.

Z is the normalising constant that makes a Boltzmann distribution a proper probability distribution. In ML, computing Z exactly is intractable for large systems — this is exactly why MCMC sampling, variational inference, and contrastive divergence in Restricted Boltzmann Machines exist. Understanding Z makes the motivation for these approximation methods immediate and obvious.

Diffusion models, energy-based models, and the free-energy principle in neuroscience all speak this language. Arriving here through Susskind and Schroeder means you understand the derivation — not just the formula.

MODULE_02 // MACKAY

Information Theory

📘
Information Theory, Inference, and Learning Algorithms
David J. C. MacKay
FREE ONLINE
▶ MacKay Lectures — YouTube ↗ ▶ Free PDF ↗
This is where physics and ML meet most explicitly. Shannon entropy is a direct extension of Boltzmann entropy — same formula, different domain. Cross-entropy loss, used to train almost every classification model in existence, is a direct consequence of information-theoretic reasoning. Bayesian inference, variational methods, KL divergence, the mathematics behind VAEs and diffusion models — all of it lives here. MacKay's book treats neural networks as a natural consequence of probabilistic reasoning, not a separate subject bolted on at the end.
[L1]Introduction to Information Theory WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 1 Introduction to Information Theory — entropy H(X), the bit, and the fundamental question: how much information does a message carry?
Ch. 2 (§2.1–2.3) Probability, entropy, and inference — joint entropy, conditional entropy H(X|Y), and the chain rule; the toolbox used throughout the book
// SOLVE
Ex. 1.1 – 1.3 ML LINK
Compute entropy for simple probability distributions. Verify H is maximised by the uniform distribution. This is the direct justification for why uniform priors are the "least informative" choice in Bayesian ML — maximum entropy = maximum uncertainty.
Ex. 2.1 – 2.4 ML LINK
Joint and conditional entropy calculations. Derive the inequality H(X,Y) ≤ H(X) + H(Y) and understand when equality holds. Independence in information theory = independence in ML features — this is the foundation of naive Bayes and factored models.
Ex. 2.9 – 2.11
Mutual information I(X;Y) — compute it for small joint distributions and verify I(X;Y) = H(X) − H(X|Y). Mutual information is used directly as a feature selection criterion and is the objective in the information bottleneck principle.
// ML CONNECTION
Shannon entropy H(X) = −∑ p log p is the same formula as the cross-entropy loss when q is replaced by the model's predicted distribution. Every classification network trained with cross-entropy loss is minimising a divergence between its output distribution and the true label distribution. This lecture is where that loss function gets its mathematical justification.
[L2]Entropy & Data Compression (I) WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 4 The Source Coding Theorem — Shannon's fundamental result: entropy is the minimum average code length; you cannot compress below H(X) bits per symbol
Ch. 5 (§5.1–5.4) Symbol codes — prefix-free codes, Kraft inequality, Huffman coding; how to actually achieve near-optimal compression
// SOLVE
Ex. 4.1 – 4.4 ML LINK
Verify the source coding theorem by computing optimal code lengths for toy distributions. Notice that − log₂ p(x) is the "surprise" of event x and also its ideal code length. This quantity − log p(x) is exactly the per-sample cross-entropy loss — the network is learning to assign short code lengths to likely outputs.
Ex. 5.1 – 5.6
Construct Huffman codes for given distributions. Verify the average length L̄ satisfies H(X) ≤ L̄ < H(X)+1. Understanding that entropy is a hard lower bound on compression is what makes the information bottleneck principle non-trivial.
Ex. 5.23 – 5.24 ML LINK
Arithmetic coding — think of the model distribution as defining a code; a better model = shorter average code. This is the connection between language model perplexity and compression: a lower-perplexity LLM literally compresses text more efficiently.
// ML CONNECTION
The minimum description length (MDL) principle — Occam's razor formalised — follows directly from source coding. A model that assigns higher probability to the data provides a shorter description of it. This is the information-theoretic justification for regularization: simpler models compress better, and compression = generalisation.
[L3]Entropy & Data Compression (II) WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 2 (§2.4–2.7) KL divergence D_KL(P‖Q) — the extra bits required when using the wrong distribution Q to encode data from P; non-symmetric and non-negative
Ch. 6 (§6.1–6.3) Stream codes and arithmetic coding — sequences of symbols, the empirical entropy, and why real-world compression must track context
// SOLVE
Ex. 2.24 – 2.26 (KL divergence) ML LINK
Compute D_KL(P‖Q) and D_KL(Q‖P) for pairs of distributions and confirm they differ. The asymmetry is not a bug — in VAEs, KL(q‖p) is chosen deliberately because it is zero-forcing (it prefers to under-estimate support rather than spread probability everywhere).
Ex. 2.27 – 2.29 ML LINK
Show that cross-entropy H(P,Q) = H(P) + D_KL(P‖Q). Minimising cross-entropy loss (H(P,Q)) when P is fixed is identical to minimising KL divergence. Write this identity out — it is the most important equation in this module.
Ex. 6.1 – 6.5
Arithmetic coding for sequences — the connection between a language model and a compressor becomes explicit here. Higher log-likelihood = lower bits-per-character = better compression.
// ML CONNECTION
KL divergence is the mathematical engine of modern generative modelling. The ELBO in VAEs is derived by decomposing log p(x) into a reconstruction term minus KL(q‖p). Diffusion model training minimises a weighted sum of KL terms at each noise level. Every time a model is trained with a "variational" objective, it is minimising a KL divergence that first appears in this lecture.
[L6]Noisy Channel Coding WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 8 Dependent random variables — conditional distributions, the data processing inequality; information cannot be created by processing
Ch. 9 Communication over a noisy channel — channel capacity C = max I(X;Y); the theoretical maximum rate of reliable transmission
Ch. 10 (§10.1–10.3) The noisy-channel coding theorem — Shannon's proof that reliable communication is possible up to capacity; random codes, typical sequences
// SOLVE
Ex. 8.1 – 8.5 (data processing inequality) ML LINK
Verify I(X;Z) ≤ I(X;Y) when Z is computed from Y. This is the theoretical foundation of the information bottleneck principle — deep networks are channels, and each layer can only reduce the mutual information between input and representation. The optimal encoder discards irrelevant bits while preserving task-relevant information.
Ex. 9.1 – 9.6 ML LINK
Compute channel capacity for the binary symmetric channel (BSC) and binary erasure channel (BEC). Map these to classification tasks: the BSC models label noise; the BEC models missing data. Channel capacity = the maximum accuracy achievable regardless of the classifier used.
Ex. 10.1 – 10.4
Typical sequences and random codes — the counting argument that makes Shannon's theorem non-obvious. Understanding why exponentially many codewords can be packed into a channel is the same intuition behind why high-dimensional spaces have room for many near-orthogonal vectors.
// ML CONNECTION
The information bottleneck (Tishby et al.) is the direct application of channel coding to deep learning: the hidden layers of a network are noisy channels, and the optimal representation minimises I(X;T) while maximising I(T;Y). The data processing inequality is the reason compression of irrelevant input information is not only possible but theoretically optimal.
[L9–10]Bayesian Inference WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 2 (§2.3) Bayes' theorem as information update — prior × likelihood → posterior; the formal machinery of belief revision
Ch. 3 More about inference — model evidence, marginal likelihood, and the Bayesian Occam's razor: complex models are automatically penalised
Ch. 28 Model comparison and Occam's Razor — the evidence framework for comparing models of different complexity; the formal version of "don't overfit"
// SOLVE
Ex. 3.1 – 3.6 ML LINK
Compute posteriors for coin-bias inference with increasing data. Watch how the posterior concentrates as N grows — this is Bayesian learning in action. The prior is the regularizer; the likelihood is the training data; the posterior is what a Bayesian neural network produces instead of a point estimate.
Ex. 28.1 – 28.3 (model evidence) ML LINK
Compute the marginal likelihood (model evidence) for polynomial regression models of different degree. The evidence automatically peaks at the correct complexity without a held-out validation set. This is the theoretical basis for Bayesian hyperparameter optimisation and for evidence-based early stopping.
Ex. 3.12 – 3.15
Sequential Bayesian updating — process data one point at a time, updating the posterior at each step. The posterior after N points is identical to the posterior from processing all N points at once. This is the justification for online learning and streaming updates in production ML systems.
// ML CONNECTION
Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) is a special case of MAP inference with a flat prior. L2 regularization is MAP with a Gaussian prior. L1 regularization is MAP with a Laplace prior. Every regularized training objective in ML is a Bayesian posterior in disguise — understanding the prior that corresponds to a given regularizer is what MacKay Chapters 2–3 make explicit.
[L14]Variational Methods WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 33 Variational methods — approximating an intractable posterior p(z|x) with a tractable family q(z); the free energy F(q) as the object to minimise
Ch. 29 (§29.1–29.3) Monte Carlo methods — sampling as an alternative to variational approximation; understanding both approaches clarifies why variational methods are preferred at scale
Ch. 27 Laplace's method — approximating posteriors with Gaussians; the simplest variational approximation and the ancestor of the Laplace approximation in Bayesian neural nets
// SOLVE
Ex. 33.1 – 33.4 ML LINK
Derive the variational free energy F(q) = ⟨log q(z)⟩ − ⟨log p(x,z)⟩ and show that minimising F(q) is equivalent to maximising the ELBO. Write out the ELBO = 𝔼_q[log p(x|z)] − D_KL(q‖p) and identify the two terms: reconstruction and regularisation. This is the VAE objective, derived from first principles.
Ex. 33.5 – 33.7 ML LINK
Mean-field variational inference — factorise q(z) = ∏ q_i(z_i) and derive the coordinate-ascent update equations. The mean-field assumption is the same factorisation used in factored variational autoencoders and in the diagonal Gaussian encoder of the standard VAE.
Ex. 27.1 – 27.3 (Laplace)
Apply Laplace's method to a 1D posterior: find the mode, compute the Hessian, fit a Gaussian. Recognise that the curvature of the loss landscape at a minimum determines the effective "posterior width" — sharp minima generalise worse than flat ones, which connects directly to the sharpness-aware minimisation (SAM) line of work.
// ML CONNECTION
Chapter 33 is the direct mathematical prerequisite for understanding VAEs, β-VAEs, and diffusion model training. The ELBO derived here is what the VAE encoder and decoder jointly optimise. The KL term in the ELBO is what forces the latent space to be a smooth, well-structured distribution — without it, the model collapses to a deterministic autoencoder.
[L15–16]Neural Networks & Boltzmann Machines WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 38–39 Introduction to neural networks and the single neuron as a classifier — the probabilistic view: a neuron computes a posterior P(class|input)
Ch. 41 Learning as inference — weight learning is Bayesian inference over parameters; the evidence framework for setting the regularisation strength automatically
Ch. 42 Hopfield networks — content-addressable memory as an energy minimisation problem; the ancestor of modern attention mechanisms
Ch. 43 Boltzmann machines — the bridge between Susskind Module 1 and deep generative models; learning = matching model statistics to data statistics
// SOLVE
Ex. 39.1 – 39.5 ML LINK
Derive the sigmoid output of a single neuron from the likelihood ratio of two Gaussians. Confirm that the logistic function is not arbitrary — it is the posterior probability under a specific generative model. This is the probabilistic justification for sigmoid activations that most DL courses skip entirely.
Ex. 41.1 – 41.4 (evidence framework) ML LINK
Use the evidence framework to find the optimal regularisation strength α for a small neural network without a validation set. Compare to the manually tuned value. The evidence framework is the theoretical basis for automatic relevance determination (ARD) and for hyperparameter optimisation in Bayesian neural networks.
Ex. 42.1 – 42.5 (Hopfield) ML LINK
Store patterns in a Hopfield network and verify retrieval under partial inputs. Compute the energy at each step and confirm it decreases monotonically. Modern Hopfield networks (Ramsauer et al., 2020) use the same energy framework with exponential interactions — and their update rule is the transformer attention mechanism.
Ex. 43.1 – 43.4 (Boltzmann)
Derive the Boltzmann machine learning rule: ΔW_ij ∝ ⟨s_i s_j⟩_data − ⟨s_i s_j⟩_model. Recognise the two terms: data statistics and model statistics. This contrastive divergence structure reappears in energy-based model training and in the negative phase of diffusion model score matching.
// ML CONNECTION
This is the payoff lecture for the entire module. The probabilistic framing of the neuron (Ch. 39), Bayesian weight learning (Ch. 41), Hopfield networks as the predecessor of attention (Ch. 42), and Boltzmann machines as the predecessor of diffusion models and energy-based models (Ch. 43) — all of modern deep learning is traceable to the ideas in these four chapters. After this lecture, the architecture choices in transformers and generative models stop being arbitrary and start being derivable.

MODULE_03 // STROGATZ

Nonlinear Dynamics & Chaos

📙
Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos
Steven H. Strogatz
▶ Strogatz Course — YouTube ↗
Neural networks are nonlinear dynamical systems. Training is a trajectory through a high-dimensional loss landscape. Fixed points, attractors, bifurcations, and stability — these aren't metaphors for what happens during training, they describe it precisely. Recurrent networks are literally dynamical systems evolving over time. Understanding when nonlinear systems stabilize, oscillate, or go chaotic builds the intuition needed to reason about why networks fail to converge, why they escape saddle points, and what a good solution actually means geometrically.
[Ch. 2]Flows on the Line — 1D Systems WATCH ↗
// READ — STROGATZ
§2.1–2.2 Geometric thinking and fixed points — draw ẋ = f(x), read off stability by inspection; the phase portrait as a complete solution
§2.4 Linear stability analysis — linearise around a fixed point; the sign of f'(x*) determines stability; the Jacobian is just the 1×1 version of what comes later
§2.7 Potentials — rewrite ẋ = −dV/dx and interpret dynamics as a ball rolling downhill; this is gradient descent, literally
// SOLVE
Ex. 2.2.1 – 2.2.9 (fixed points) ML LINK
For each ODE sketch the phase portrait and classify every fixed point as stable, unstable, or half-stable. A stable fixed point is a loss minimum; an unstable one is a saddle or maximum. The geometric picture here is the 1D version of the loss landscape reasoning used in all of Chapter 8 (optimisation) of Goodfellow et al.
Ex. 2.7.1 – 2.7.5 (potentials) ML LINK
Rewrite each system as gradient descent on a potential V(x). Confirm that trajectories always flow downhill and that stable fixed points are potential minima. This is gradient descent on a 1D loss landscape — write the update rule Δx = −η f'(x) and recognise it as the Euler method applied to ẋ = −dV/dx.
Ex. 2.3.1 – 2.3.4 (population growth)
Logistic growth model — the same sigmoid saturation that appears in neural network activation functions. The carrying capacity K is a stable fixed point; verify it using linear stability analysis.
// ML CONNECTION
Gradient descent is the Euler discretisation of ẋ = −∇L(x). Every concept from this chapter has a direct ML translation: fixed point = converged model, stable fixed point = local minimum, unstable fixed point = saddle point, basin of attraction = region where a given optimiser converges to a given minimum. The potential V(x) is the loss function. The 1D picture is the foundation for all higher-dimensional loss landscape intuition.
[Ch. 3]Bifurcations WATCH ↗
// READ — STROGATZ
§3.1 Saddle-node bifurcation — two fixed points collide and annihilate as a parameter crosses a threshold; the canonical "phase transition" in a 1D system
§3.4 Pitchfork bifurcation — one stable fixed point splits into two as a parameter increases; symmetry breaking at its simplest
§3.6 Imperfect bifurcations and catastrophes — small perturbations to a symmetric system create qualitatively different behaviour; robustness and sensitivity to hyperparameters
// SOLVE
Ex. 3.1.1 – 3.1.6 (saddle-node) ML LINK
Locate the bifurcation point r_c for ẋ = r + x². For r > r_c there are no fixed points — the system has no equilibrium. This models a network that fails to converge as a hyperparameter (learning rate, weight decay) crosses a critical threshold. The sudden loss of stability is exactly what happens when the learning rate is too large.
Ex. 3.4.1 – 3.4.5 (pitchfork) ML LINK
Trace the pitchfork bifurcation for ẋ = rx − x³. Below r = 0 there is one stable fixed point; above it the system has two. This is spontaneous symmetry breaking — the same mechanism that creates two competing modes in a bimodal latent space, or two degenerate solutions in an underdetermined network.
Ex. 3.6.1 – 3.6.3 (catastrophes)
Imperfect pitchfork: add a small asymmetry h to the system. The clean bifurcation becomes a smooth curve — one branch disappears. This is the ML intuition for why small regularisation (h ≠ 0) can break the degeneracy between two equally good solutions and steer the optimiser to a preferred minimum.
// ML CONNECTION
Phase transitions in training — sudden changes in loss or accuracy as a hyperparameter varies — are bifurcations. Grokking (delayed generalisation), double descent, and the sharp transition from memorisation to generalisation in large language models are all bifurcation phenomena. Strogatz Chapter 3 gives the exact vocabulary and tools needed to reason about them without hand-waving.
[Ch. 5–6]Linear Systems & Phase Plane WATCH ↗
// READ — STROGATZ
§5.1–5.2 Classification of 2D linear systems — eigenvalues determine everything: nodes, spirals, centres, saddles; the full taxonomy from trace and determinant
§6.3 Fixed points and linearisation — the Jacobian matrix at a fixed point determines local stability; this is what the Hessian does at a loss minimum
§6.5 Conservative systems — energy is preserved; trajectories form closed orbits; understanding when a system conserves a quantity is prerequisite for Hamiltonian mechanics (Module 6)
// SOLVE
Ex. 5.1.1 – 5.1.10 (eigenvalue classification) ML LINK
Classify each 2×2 system by computing eigenvalues: stable node (both negative), saddle (opposite signs), stable spiral (complex with negative real part). Map each case to a training scenario: stable node = clean convergence, saddle = stuck training, spiral = oscillating loss. The trace-determinant plane is the 2D loss landscape.
Ex. 6.3.1 – 6.3.8 (Jacobian / linearisation) ML LINK
Compute the Jacobian at each fixed point of a nonlinear system and classify its stability. This is exactly what second-order optimisers (Newton's method, natural gradient) do at each step — they linearise the loss landscape using the Hessian and step toward the stable fixed point of the linearised system.
Ex. 6.4.1 – 6.4.5 (rabbits vs. sheep)
The Lotka-Volterra competition model — two species competing for resources. Identify all fixed points and their stability; determine which species survives from the initial conditions. This is the ML intuition for competing optima: two attractors in the loss landscape, and the outcome depends entirely on initialisation.
// ML CONNECTION
The Hessian of the loss at a minimum is the Jacobian of the gradient flow. Its eigenvalues determine the local geometry: positive eigenvalues = stable directions (curvature), near-zero eigenvalues = flat directions (plateaus), negative eigenvalues = saddle directions (escape routes). Understanding eigenvalue structure is why sharpness-aware minimisation (SAM) and natural gradient descent work — they reshape the local geometry to avoid sharp minima.
[Ch. 7]Limit Cycles WATCH ↗
// READ — STROGATZ
§7.0–7.1 Introduction and examples — isolated closed orbits that nearby trajectories spiral toward; stable periodic behaviour that is qualitatively different from fixed-point convergence
§7.2 Ruling out closed orbits — Bendixson's criterion; understanding why certain systems cannot oscillate is as important as knowing when they can
§7.5 Relaxation oscillations — systems that build tension slowly then release rapidly; the van der Pol oscillator as a prototype of spiking neurons and periodic training dynamics
// SOLVE
Ex. 7.2.1 – 7.2.7 (ruling out limit cycles) ML LINK
Apply Bendixson's criterion to confirm certain systems have no closed orbits. The ML translation: these theorems tell you when an optimiser is guaranteed to converge to a fixed point rather than cycling. SGD with constant learning rate on a non-convex loss can exhibit limit-cycle-like behaviour — understanding when this is and isn't possible is a key stability diagnostic.
Ex. 7.5.1 – 7.5.4 (van der Pol / relaxation) ML LINK
Simulate the van der Pol oscillator numerically; observe the limit cycle in phase space and the slow-fast separation in time. The slow buildup / fast release is the mechanism behind bursting neurons and is the qualitative behaviour of loss spikes observed during LLM training — sudden destabilisation followed by rapid recovery.
Ex. 7.1.1 – 7.1.5
Find and classify limit cycles for given polar-coordinate systems. Verify that the cycle is stable by checking nearby trajectories spiral inward. This is the RNN stability condition: a stable limit cycle = sustained oscillation (periodic memory); an unstable one = the network cannot maintain periodic patterns.
// ML CONNECTION
Recurrent neural networks trained on periodic tasks (music generation, time-series forecasting, motor control) must learn to produce limit cycles in their hidden-state dynamics. Vanishing gradients occur because the Jacobian of the RNN map at a stable fixed point has spectral radius < 1 — the system damps all perturbations. Limit cycles require spectral radius = 1. This is why LSTM and GRU gates are designed to let the spectral radius stay near 1 across many time steps.
[Ch. 8]Bifurcations Revisited — Hopf & Global WATCH ↗
// READ — STROGATZ
§8.1 Saddle-node, transcritical, and pitchfork in 2D — the 1D bifurcations reappear in higher-dimensional systems; same algebra, richer geometry
§8.2 Hopf bifurcation — a fixed point loses stability and a limit cycle is born; the transition from convergence to oscillation as a single parameter crosses a threshold

Classical Mechanics
John R. Taylor
▶ Susskind L1 — State Space ↗ ▶ Susskind L4 — Lagrangian ↗ ▶ Susskind L7 — Hamiltonian ↗
The Hamiltonian formulation of mechanics — energy as the central object, phase space as the arena — is the conceptual ancestor of several ideas now appearing at the frontier of deep learning. Hamiltonian Monte Carlo is a direct application used in Bayesian inference. Hamiltonian Neural Networks learn to respect conservation laws in physical simulations. The Lagrangian framework, built on the principle of least action, mirrors how optimisation finds paths of minimal cost through parameter space. This module closes the loop — the physicist's toolkit becomes the ML practitioner's toolkit.

Ex. 4.3 – 4.6 (gradient of potential) ML LINK
Compute F = −∇V for several 2D potential surfaces. Find the equilibrium points by setting ∇V = 0 and classify them using the Hessian ∂²V/∂xᵢ∂xⱼ. This is the loss landscape in coordinates: equilibria are critical points, positive-definite Hessian = local minimum, indefinite Hessian = saddle. The force pointing toward minima is the negative gradient — the update direction of gradient descent.

Solve for the steady-state amplitude A(ω) of the driven damped oscillator. Plot |A(ω)|² as a function of driving frequency. Identify the resonance peak and the bandwidth. Fourier analysis is the mathematical foundation of the attention mechanism's positional encodings, of the S4/Mamba state space models operating in the frequency domain, and of why convolutional networks detect multi-scale frequency features.
// ML CONNECTION
The potential energy surface V(x) and the loss landscape L(θ) are the same mathematical object. A particle rolling under −∇V is gradient descent. Damping = momentum decay (the β parameter in Adam). Driving frequency = learning rate oscillation in cyclical schedules. Resonance = the catastrophic instability that occurs when a periodic learning rate matches the natural frequency of the loss curvature. Chapter 5 gives the exact vocabulary to reason about these phenomena quantitatively.

Derive the equation of motion for a free particle, a pendulum, and a bead on a wire by applying the EL equation to each Lagrangian. // ML CONNECTION
The calculus of variations is the mathematical foundation for three important ideas in modern ML: (1) natural gradient descent, which finds the geodesic in the statistical manifold of model distributions; (2) optimal transport, which minimises the "action" of moving probability mass between distributions — used in Wasserstein GANs and diffusion model theory; (3) the path integral formulation of deep learning, where training trajectories are weighted by exp(−S[θ(t)]) and the dominant path minimises the action. Chapter 6 is where "learning as optimisation" becomes "learning as physics."

Ex. 11.1 – 11.4 (normal modes) ML LINK
Find the normal mode frequencies and eigenvectors of a 2-mass spring system. Verify that in normal coordinates the equations of motion decouple completely. The Hessian of the loss at a minimum plays the role of the stiffness matrix K — its eigenvectors are the principal curvature directions. The normal modes of the loss are the directions along which gradient descent converges independently, at rates determined by the eigenvalues. This is the physics of the condition number.
Ex. 11.5 – 11.6 (general eigenvalue problem) ML LINK
Solve Kv = ω²Mv for a 3-oscillator chain. Diagonalise in the eigenbasis and verify decoupling. The ratio ω_max/ω_min is the condition number of the system. A poorly conditioned loss landscape (large condition number) is a system with widely separated normal mode frequencies — gradient descent converges at the rate of the slowest mode. Preconditioning is the ML equivalent of rescaling to equalise all mode frequencies.
Ex. 11.3 (weakly coupled oscillators)
Two nearly identical oscillators — energy sloshes back and forth between them with a beat frequency ω_beat = |ω₁ − ω₂|. // ML CONNECTION
PCA is normal mode analysis applied to data: find the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix (the data's "stiffness matrix") and project onto the principal axes. The eigenvalues are the mode frequencies — high eigenvalue = high-variance direction = fast mode. Truncating to the top k eigenvectors is retaining only the slow (large-amplitude) normal modes. Every linear dimensionality reduction method is normal mode analysis; every second-order optimiser is solving the normal mode problem of the loss Hessian.

Ex. 13.2 – 13.5 (Hamilton's equations) ML LINK
Convert the Lagrangian of the harmonic oscillator and the pendulum to the Hamiltonian formulation. Write Hamilton's equations and verify they match Newton's second law. The Hamiltonian H(q,p) = T + V is the total energy — conserved exactly. In Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, the position q is the model parameter and the momentum p is an auxiliary variable; H governs the proposal trajectory, ensuring it explores the target distribution efficiently.
Ex. 13.6.1 – 13.6.4 (phase-space orbits) ML LINK
Plot the phase portrait (q,p) of the harmonic oscillator, the pendulum, and a double-well potential. Identify separatrices and basins of attraction. Ex. 13.7.1 – 13.7.3 (Liouville's theorem) ML LINK
Verify Liouville's theorem for a 2D Hamiltonian system: show that the Jacobian of the flow map has determinant 1 at all times. Contrast with a dissipative system (∇·F < 0) where volumes contract. Hamiltonian Monte Carlo exploits Liouville's theorem to construct a Metropolis proposal that is automatically volume-preserving — this is why it has a higher acceptance rate than random-walk proposals and why symplectic integrators (leapfrog) are required.
// ML CONNECTION
Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) is the direct application of Chapter 13 to Bayesian inference. The target posterior π(θ) ∝ exp(−U(θ)) defines the potential; an auxiliary momentum p ~ N(0,I) defines the kinetic energy; the Hamiltonian H(θ,p) = U(θ) + p²/2 governs the proposal trajectory. Liouville's theorem guarantees volume preservation, making the Metropolis acceptance rate high. The leapfrog integrator preserves the symplectic structure of Hamilton's equations — use any other integrator and Liouville's theorem breaks, destroying the efficiency advantage of HMC.
