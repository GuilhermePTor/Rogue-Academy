MODULE_01 // THEORETICAL_MINIMUM

Statistical Mechanics

📗
An Introduction to Thermal Physics
Daniel V. Schroeder
▶ Susskind — Theoretical Minimum ↗
Statistical mechanics is where ML borrows most heavily from physics. The Boltzmann distribution is the mathematical ancestor of softmax. Partition functions appear throughout probabilistic modelling. Free energy minimization is what variational inference is actually doing. The entropy studied here is the same entropy that defines information content, uncertainty in predictions, and regularization in neural networks. After this module, gradient descent starts to look like a system seeking its lowest energy state — because formally, it is.
[L1]Probability & Entropy WATCH ↗
// READ — SCHROEDER
§2.1Two-state systems — the simplest probability space; entropy as log of microstates
§2.2Einstein solid — practice counting multiplicities before they get large
§2.3Interacting systems — where entropy maximisation first appears
§2.4Stirling's approximation — essential tool for all large-N calculations ahead
§2.6Entropy — formal definition; reversible vs. irreversible mirrors Susskind's info-conservation argument
// SOLVE
Problems 2.1 – 2.3ML LINK
Counting arrangements of coins and spins. Build the combinatorial intuition that Ω → ln Ω → entropy. This is the exact same operation as computing the entropy of a probability distribution in information theory — and the foundation of cross-entropy loss.
Problem 2.9
Einstein solid multiplicities for small N — compute by hand to feel the exponential growth before applying Stirling.
Problems 2.22 – 2.24ML LINK
Entropy of large two-state systems and entropy of mixing. Entropy of mixing is the direct ancestor of KL divergence — the cost of using the wrong probability distribution to describe data.
// ML CONNECTION
Shannon entropy and thermodynamic entropy are the same quantity up to the constant k₂. Every time you minimise cross-entropy loss in a neural network you are minimising a thermodynamic quantity. Understanding why entropy measures uncertainty — not just that it does — is what makes KL divergence and the information bottleneck principle legible later.
[L3]Boltzmann Distribution WATCH ↗
// READ — SCHROEDER
§3.1Statistical definition of temperature — must be solid before the reservoir argument makes sense

§6.1The Boltzmann factor — the core derivation; do not skip any steps
§3.3Paramagnetism (thermodynamic approach) — first application; will be revisited in L4 via partition functions
// SOLVE
Problems 3.1 – 3.5
Temperature from the entropy derivative. Solidify ∂S/∂U = 1/T before you use it in the Boltzmann derivation.
Problems 6.1 – 6.3ML LINK
Computing Boltzmann factors for a two-state system. Write out both probabilities P₁ and P₂, verify they sum to 1, compute their ratio. This is the softmax function with two classes.
Problem 6.6ML LINK
Boltzmann factors for a three-level system — now it is a softmax with three classes. The inverse temperature β = 1/kT is exactly the temperature scaling used in attention and in language model sampling.
// ML CONNECTION
The Boltzmann distribution is the softmax function. P(state i) = e−βEᵢ/Z is identical in form to softmax(logits/T). The "temperature" T in language model sampling is the literal thermodynamic temperature. Energy-based models, Gibbs sampling, simulated annealing, and the attention mechanism all trace back to this derivation.
[L4]Boltzmann Distribution & Partition Functions WATCH ↗
// READ — SCHROEDER
§6.2Average values from the partition function — the ∂ln Z/∂β trick is the key technique to master
§6.3Paramagnet via partition function — worked example; compare to §3.3 to see both approaches converge
§6.4Rotation and vibration — optional, but good extra partition function practice
// SOLVE
Problems 6.13 – 6.16ML LINK
Derive ⟨E⟩ = −∂ln Z/∂β for the two-state and harmonic oscillator systems. This is the log-derivative trick — the same mathematical move used in policy gradient methods (REINFORCE) in reinforcement learning.
Problem 6.18ML LINK
Partition function for the paramagnet in an external field. Compute ⟨M⟩ and plot vs. T. This is the activation function of a biological neuron — the sigmoid and tanh — derived from first principles.
Problems 6.20 – 6.22
Factoring the partition function for independent subsystems (Z = Z₁ × Z₂). Understanding when systems factor is central to mean field approximations and to why factored variational distributions work at all.
// ML CONNECTION
Z is the normalising constant that makes a Boltzmann distribution a proper probability distribution. In ML, computing Z exactly is intractable for large systems — this is exactly why MCMC sampling, variational inference, and contrastive divergence in Restricted Boltzmann Machines exist.
[L5]Helmholtz Free Energy & Ideal Gas WATCH ↗
// READ — SCHROEDER

§5.2Helmholtz free energy F = U − TS — derive F = −kT ln Z here; it is the payoff of Lectures 3–4
§2.5Ideal gas multiplicity — read before §6.5; bridges the entropy-counting approach to partition functions
§6.5Ideal gas via partition function — shows how PV = nRT drops out of ∂F/∂V

Problems 5.1 – 5.4ML LINK
Verify F = U − TS for simple systems and show equilibrium minimises F at fixed T. This variational principle is the foundation of the ELBO in variational autoencoders: minimising free energy = maximising a lower bound on log-likelihood.
Problem 5.13ML LINK
Free energy of a two-state system — compute F(T) and find equilibrium as a function of temperature. This is conceptually identical to temperature in simulated annealing and to noise schedules in diffusion models.
Problems 6.29 – 6.31
Maxwell speed distribution from the partition function — a full worked example of deriving a continuous probability distribution over states from first principles.
// ML CONNECTION
The Helmholtz free energy F = U − TS is the variational free energy that Variational Autoencoders minimise. The ELBO = 𝔼[log p(x|z)] − KL(q‖p) is exactly −F in disguise: reconstruction quality (U) minus an entropy penalty (TS).

MODULE_02 // MACKAY

Information Theory

📘
Information Theory, Inference, and Learning Algorithms
David J. C. MacKay
FREE ONLINE
▶ MacKay Lectures — YouTube ↗ ▶ Free PDF ↗
This is where physics and ML meet most explicitly. Shannon entropy is a direct extension of Boltzmann entropy — same formula, different domain. Cross-entropy loss, used to train almost every classification model in existence, is a direct consequence of information-theoretic reasoning. Bayesian inference, variational methods, KL divergence, the mathematics behind VAEs and diffusion models — all of it lives here. MacKay's book treats neural networks as a natural consequence of probabilistic reasoning, not a separate subject bolted on at the end.
[L1]Introduction to Information Theory WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 1Introduction to Information Theory — entropy H(X), the bit, and the fundamental question: how much information does a message carry?
Ch. 2 (§2.1–2.3)Probability, entropy, and inference — joint entropy, conditional entropy H(X|Y), and the chain rule
// SOLVE
Ex. 1.1 – 1.3ML LINK
Compute entropy for simple probability distributions. Verify H is maximised by the uniform distribution. This is the direct justification for why uniform priors are the "least informative" choice in Bayesian ML.
Ex. 2.1 – 2.4ML LINK
Joint and conditional entropy calculations. Derive H(X,Y) ≤ H(X) + H(Y) and understand when equality holds. Independence in information theory = independence in ML features.
Ex. 2.9 – 2.11
Mutual information I(X;Y) — compute it for small joint distributions. Mutual information is used directly as a feature selection criterion and is the objective in the information bottleneck principle.
// ML CONNECTION
Shannon entropy H(X) = −∑ p log p is the same formula as the cross-entropy loss when q is replaced by the model's predicted distribution. Every classification network trained with cross-entropy loss is minimising a divergence between its output distribution and the true label distribution.
[L2]Entropy & Data Compression (I) WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 4The Source Coding Theorem — entropy is the minimum average code length; you cannot compress below H(X) bits per symbol
Ch. 5 (§5.1–5.4)Symbol codes — prefix-free codes, Kraft inequality, Huffman coding
// SOLVE
Ex. 4.1 – 4.4ML LINK
Verify the source coding theorem by computing optimal code lengths for toy distributions. Notice that − log₂ p(x) is the "surprise" and also its ideal code length. This quantity is exactly the per-sample cross-entropy loss.
Ex. 5.1 – 5.6
Construct Huffman codes for given distributions. Verify the average length satisfies H(X) ≤ L̄ < H(X)+1.
Ex. 5.23 – 5.24ML LINK
Arithmetic coding — a better model = shorter average code. This is the connection between language model perplexity and compression: a lower-perplexity LLM literally compresses text more efficiently.
// ML CONNECTION
The minimum description length (MDL) principle follows directly from source coding. A model that assigns higher probability to the data provides a shorter description of it. This is the information-theoretic justification for regularization: simpler models compress better, and compression = generalisation.
[L3]Entropy & Data Compression (II) — KL Divergence WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 2 (§2.4–2.7)KL divergence D_KL(P‖Q) — the extra bits required when using the wrong distribution Q to encode data from P
Ch. 6 (§6.1–6.3)Stream codes and arithmetic coding — sequences of symbols, the empirical entropy
// SOLVE
Ex. 2.24 – 2.26 (KL divergence)ML LINK
Compute D_KL(P‖Q) and D_KL(Q‖P) for pairs of distributions and confirm they differ. The asymmetry is not a bug — in VAEs, KL(q‖p) is chosen deliberately because it is zero-forcing.
Ex. 2.27 – 2.29ML LINK
Show that cross-entropy H(P,Q) = H(P) + D_KL(P‖Q). Minimising cross-entropy loss when P is fixed is identical to minimising KL divergence. Write this identity out — it is the most important equation in this module.
Ex. 6.1 – 6.5
Arithmetic coding for sequences — higher log-likelihood = lower bits-per-character = better compression.
// ML CONNECTION
KL divergence is the mathematical engine of modern generative modelling. The ELBO in VAEs is derived by decomposing log p(x) into a reconstruction term minus KL(q‖p). Diffusion model training minimises a weighted sum of KL terms at each noise level.
[L14]Variational Methods WATCH ↗
// READ — MACKAY
Ch. 33Variational methods — approximating an intractable posterior p(z|x) with a tractable family q(z); the free energy F(q) as the object to minimise
Ch. 29 (§29.1–29.3)Monte Carlo methods — sampling as an alternative to variational approximation
Ch. 27Laplace's method — approximating posteriors with Gaussians; the simplest variational approximation
// SOLVE
Ex. 33.1 – 33.4ML LINK
Derive the variational free energy F(q) and show that minimising F(q) is equivalent to maximising the ELBO = 𝔼_q[log p(x|z)] − D_KL(q‖p). This is the VAE objective, derived from first principles.
Ex. 33.5 – 33.7ML LINK
Mean-field variational inference — factorise q(z) = ∏ q_i(z_i) and derive the coordinate-ascent updates. The mean-field assumption is the same factorisation used in the diagonal Gaussian encoder of the standard VAE.
// ML CONNECTION
Chapter 33 is the direct mathematical prerequisite for understanding VAEs, β-VAEs, and diffusion model training. The KL term in the ELBO forces the latent space to be a smooth, well-structured distribution — without it, the model collapses to a deterministic autoencoder.
// AI_CONNECTIONS

MODULE_03 // STROGATZ

Nonlinear Dynamics & Chaos

📙
Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos
Steven H. Strogatz
▶ Strogatz Course — YouTube ↗
Neural networks are nonlinear dynamical systems. Training is a trajectory through a high-dimensional loss landscape. Fixed points, attractors, bifurcations, and stability — these aren't metaphors for what happens during training, they describe it precisely. Recurrent networks are literally dynamical systems evolving over time. Understanding when nonlinear systems stabilize, oscillate, or go chaotic builds the intuition needed to reason about why networks fail to converge, why they escape saddle points, and what a good solution actually means geometrically.
[Ch. 2]Flows on the Line — 1D Systems WATCH ↗
// READ — STROGATZ
§2.1–2.2Geometric thinking and fixed points — draw ẋ = f(x), read off stability by inspection
§2.4Linear stability analysis — linearise around a fixed point; the sign of f'(x*) determines stability
§2.7Potentials — rewrite ẋ = −dV/dx and interpret dynamics as a ball rolling downhill; this is gradient descent, literally
// SOLVE
Ex. 2.2.1 – 2.2.9 (fixed points)ML LINK
For each ODE sketch the phase portrait and classify every fixed point. A stable fixed point is a loss minimum; an unstable one is a saddle or maximum.
Ex. 2.7.1 – 2.7.5 (potentials)ML LINK
Rewrite each system as gradient descent on a potential V(x). This is gradient descent on a 1D loss landscape — write the update rule Δx = −η f'(x) and recognise it as the Euler method applied to ẋ = −dV/dx.
// ML CONNECTION
Gradient descent is the Euler discretisation of ẋ = −∇L(x). Every concept from this chapter has a direct ML translation: fixed point = converged model, stable fixed point = local minimum, unstable fixed point = saddle point, basin of attraction = region where a given optimiser converges to a given minimum.
[Ch. 3]Bifurcations WATCH ↗
// READ — STROGATZ
§3.1Saddle-node bifurcation — two fixed points collide and annihilate; the canonical "phase transition" in a 1D system
§3.4Pitchfork bifurcation — one stable fixed point splits into two; symmetry breaking at its simplest
§3.6Imperfect bifurcations and catastrophes — small perturbations create qualitatively different behaviour
// SOLVE
Ex. 3.1.1 – 3.1.6 (saddle-node)ML LINK
Locate the bifurcation point r_c for ẋ = r + x². The sudden loss of stability is exactly what happens when the learning rate is too large.
Ex. 3.4.1 – 3.4.5 (pitchfork)ML LINK
Trace the pitchfork bifurcation for ẋ = rx − x³. This is spontaneous symmetry breaking — the same mechanism that creates two competing modes in a bimodal latent space.
// ML CONNECTION
Phase transitions in training — sudden changes in loss or accuracy as a hyperparameter varies — are bifurcations. Grokking, double descent, and the sharp transition from memorisation to generalisation in LLMs are all bifurcation phenomena.
[Ch. 9–10]Chaos — Lorenz Equations & 1D Maps WATCH ↗
// READ — STROGATZ
§9.2–9.3Simple properties of the Lorenz equations and chaos on a strange attractor — sensitivity to initial conditions
§10.1–10.3Fixed points and cobwebs, logistic map — the simplest route to chaos via period doubling
§10.5Lyapunov exponent — positive = chaotic; negative = stable convergence; zero = marginally stable
// SOLVE
Ex. 10.5.1 – 10.5.4 (Lyapunov exponent)ML LINK
Compute the Lyapunov exponent λ for the logistic map. The Lyapunov exponent of an RNN's weight matrix determines whether gradients vanish (λ < 0), explode (λ > 0), or propagate (λ ≈ 0). Gradient clipping is a hard fix; careful initialisation targets λ ≈ 0 by design.
// ML CONNECTION
The vanishing and exploding gradient problems are Lyapunov exponent phenomena. Residual connections (ResNets) keep the Lyapunov exponent near zero by construction, placing the network at the "edge of chaos" where information propagates through arbitrary depth.
// AI_CONNECTIONS
Gradient descent as gradient flow Loss landscape & fixed points Bifurcations & phase transitions Hopf → learning rate instability Lyapunov → vanishing gradients RNNs as dynamical systems Edge of chaos & ResNets Manifold hypothesis
MODULE_04 // HYNDMAN & ATHANASOPOULOS

Forecasting — Applied Time Series

🐍
Forecasting: Principles & Practice (Python edition)
Rob J. Hyndman & George Athanasopoulos
FREE ONLINE
▶ FPPy Online Book ↗ ▶ Lecture Playlist ↗ ▶ GitHub — Notebooks ↗
This is where physical systems thinking gets applied computationally. Time series are the observable outputs of dynamical systems — everything from climate to financial markets to neural signals. Decomposition, smoothing, ARIMA, and state-space models are all grounded in the same reasoning used to analyse physical processes evolving over time. The transition to neural forecasting (N-BEATS, TFT, foundation models) shows how deep learning enters as a generalisation of classical methods — not a replacement, but an extension of the same underlying logic. After Strogatz (Module 3), these methods stop being black boxes and start being recognisable as discretised dynamical systems.
[Ch. 1–2]Getting Started & Time Series Graphics WATCH ↗
// READ — FPPPY
Ch. 1Getting started — what forecasting is and isn't; the forecasting task as estimating a conditional distribution P(y_{t+h} | y_{1:t}); this is probabilistic inference, not curve fitting
Ch. 2Time series graphics — tsibble objects, seasonal plots, lag plots, autocorrelation function (ACF); visualising the temporal dependence structure before modelling it
// SOLVE
Ex. 2.1 – 2.5 (ACF plots)ML LINK
Compute and plot the ACF of several real time series (airline passengers, retail sales, temperature). Identify the lag structure: slow decay = trend, seasonal spikes = periodicity, rapid cutoff = weak dependence. The ACF is the empirical autocorrelation — the same structure that attention mechanisms learn to detect in sequences, and that S4/Mamba state space models encode in their transition matrices.
Ex. 2.6 – 2.9 (seasonal plots)
Produce seasonal and subseries plots for a monthly series. Confirm the period visually. Identify which months drive most of the variance. This visual decomposition is the intuition behind learned positional and seasonal embeddings in transformer-based forecasters.
// ML CONNECTION
A time series is a sequence of observations from an underlying dynamical system (Strogatz Module 3). The ACF measures how much information past observations carry about future ones — this is mutual information I(y_t; y_{t-k}) in the Gaussian case. High ACF at lag k means the series has memory of depth k, which directly sets the minimum context window length a neural forecaster needs.
[Ch. 3–4]Decomposition & Time Series Features WATCH ↗
// READ — FPPPY
Ch. 3Time series decomposition — additive and multiplicative decompositions y_t = T_t + S_t + R_t; STL decomposition using loess; isolating trend, seasonality, and remainder for independent analysis
Ch. 4Time series features — summary statistics: strength of trend, seasonality, entropy, spectral features; the feature vector as a fixed-size representation of a variable-length sequence
// SOLVE
Ex. 3.1 – 3.5 (STL decomposition)ML LINK
Apply STL decomposition to monthly retail sales and quarterly GDP. Extract the remainder R_t. Test whether R_t is white noise using the Ljung-Box statistic. A non-white remainder means the model has not captured all structure — the remainder is the signal a neural network should be asked to learn, not the full raw series. Remove what you understand analytically; model what remains.
Ex. 4.1 – 4.5 (feature extraction)ML LINK
Compute the feature matrix for a panel of 100 time series using Ch. 4 features (trend strength, seasonality strength, ACF1, spectral entropy). Visualise in 2D with PCA. Cluster similar series. This is the standard workflow for large-scale forecasting — the same pipeline used in M4 and M5 competition winners.
// ML CONNECTION
STL decomposition is a physics-informed preprocessing step: it separates deterministic components (trend, seasonality) that can be modelled analytically from the stochastic remainder requiring learning. The spectral entropy feature in Ch. 4 is the same Shannon entropy from MacKay Module 2, applied to the power spectrum of a time series.
[Ch. 5–6]The Forecaster's Toolbox & Judgmental Forecasts WATCH ↗
// READ — FPPPY
Ch. 5The forecaster's toolbox — benchmark methods (naïve, seasonal naïve, drift, mean); forecast distributions and prediction intervals; residual diagnostics; train/test split for time series
Ch. 6Judgmental forecasts — structured expert elicitation, the Delphi method, combining statistical and judgmental forecasts; the limits of model-based approaches when data is scarce
// SOLVE
Ex. 5.1 – 5.7 (benchmarks + prediction intervals)ML LINK
Fit all four benchmark methods to an airline passenger series. Compute 80%/95% prediction intervals. Evaluate on a held-out test set using RMSE and MASE. The critical lesson: always beat a naïve baseline before claiming a complex model adds value. Most neural forecasters in the literature fail to beat seasonal naïve on standard benchmarks — the naïve baseline is the null hypothesis.
Ex. 5.8 – 5.11 (residual diagnostics)
Inspect residuals of each benchmark: plot ACF of residuals, apply the Ljung-Box test, check normality. A good model has white-noise residuals. Residual ACF directly shows what structure remains for a neural network to learn.
// ML CONNECTION
Prediction intervals are the frequentist version of the Bayesian posterior predictive distribution from MacKay Module 2. Neural forecasters that output quantiles (quantile regression) or full distributions are doing exactly what Ch. 5 describes, but with a learned conditional distribution rather than a Gaussian approximation.
[Ch. 7–8]Time Series Regression & Exponential Smoothing (ETS) WATCH ↗
// READ — FPPPY
Ch. 7Time series regression — regression with time series predictors; spurious regression and cointegration; Fourier terms for complex seasonality
Ch. 8 (§8.1–8.4)Simple, double, and Holt's exponential smoothing — the weighted average of past observations with exponentially decaying weights; the smoothing parameter α as the memory decay rate
Ch. 8 (§8.5–8.7)Holt-Winters seasonal method and ETS state-space formulation — error, trend, seasonality modelled jointly; maximum likelihood estimation of smoothing parameters
// SOLVE
Ex. 8.1 – 8.5 (smoothing parameter)ML LINK
Fit simple exponential smoothing with α = 0.1, 0.5, 0.9 and compare forecasts. The smoothing parameter α is the inverse memory constant — it is the discrete-time version of the decay rate in a first-order ODE (Strogatz Ch. 2). An ETS model is a discretised linear dynamical system whose parameters are learned by maximum likelihood.
Ex. 8.6 – 8.9 (ETS state-space formulation)ML LINK
Write the ETS(A,A,A) model in state-space form: state vector x_t = (l_t, b_t, s_t), transition matrix F, observation equation. This is a Kalman filter — the linear Gaussian special case of the Dynamic Bayesian Network from Koller Module 5 (Ch. 6). ETS is HMM with Gaussian emissions and a linear transition, fitted by the Kalman recursion.
Ex. 7.5 – 7.8 (Fourier terms for seasonality)
Add K pairs of Fourier sine/cosine terms to a regression model. Compare models for K = 1, 3, 5 using AIC. The Fourier representation of seasonality is the same spectral decomposition from Taylor Module 6 (§5.8) — the series is decomposed into normal modes and the regression selects which modes carry signal.
// ML CONNECTION
Exponential smoothing is a learnable first-order IIR filter — the exact same structure as a GRU or LSTM cell with a single gate. The smoothing parameter α is the gate value; (1−α) is the forget factor. Modern SSMs (S4, Mamba) are the deep, multi-state generalisations of exactly this structure: they learn banks of ETS-like filters in parallel, one per latent state dimension.
[Ch. 9–10]ARIMA Models & Dynamic Regression WATCH ↗
// READ — FPPPY
Ch. 9 (§9.1–9.4)Stationarity, differencing, and the ARIMA model — AR(p) and MA(q) polynomials; the characteristic roots of the AR polynomial determine stability
Ch. 9 (§9.5–9.8)Model selection via ACF/PACF and information criteria; seasonal ARIMA (SARIMA); automatic model selection with auto_arima
Ch. 10Dynamic regression models — ARIMAX; regression with ARIMA errors; transfer function models; interventions and outliers
// SOLVE
Ex. 9.1 – 9.6 (AR characteristic roots)ML LINK
For AR(2) with coefficients φ₁ and φ₂, compute the characteristic roots of 1 − φ₁B − φ₂B² = 0. Confirm stationarity iff all roots lie outside the unit circle. Map this to the RNN stability condition from Strogatz: the roots of the AR polynomial are the eigenvalues of the companion matrix — identical to the Jacobian eigenvalue stability condition for a recurrent network.
Ex. 9.7 – 9.10 (SARIMA identification)ML LINK
Identify the SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)[m] order using ACF and PACF plots. Select the best specification using AIC. AIC = −2 log-likelihood + 2k is the information-theoretic model selection criterion from MacKay Module 2 (Ch. 28) — Bayesian Occam's razor with a flat prior approximation. Choosing lag order by AIC is choosing the model complexity that minimises free energy.
Ex. 10.1 – 10.5 (dynamic regression / ARIMAX)ML LINK
Fit a dynamic regression model for electricity demand using temperature as an exogenous regressor with lagged effects. This is the linear version of what a Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) does: combine known future covariates with autoregressive dynamics to produce a conditional forecast distribution.
// ML CONNECTION
An ARIMA(p,d,q) model is a linear recurrent network. The AR coefficients are the recurrent weights; the MA coefficients are the input-to-output weights. The stationarity condition (roots outside the unit circle) is the stability condition for a linear RNN (spectral radius < 1). Fitting ARIMA is training this linear RNN by maximum likelihood — the Kalman filter computes the likelihood exactly in O(T) time, which is the linear-algebra version of what transformers do in O(T²) for nonlinear sequence modelling.
[Ch. 11–13]Hierarchical Forecasting, Advanced Methods & Practical Issues WATCH ↗
// READ — FPPPY
Ch. 11Forecasting hierarchical and grouped time series — coherence constraints: forecasts at all levels must sum to the aggregate; MinT reconciliation minimises total error subject to summation constraints
Ch. 12Advanced forecasting methods — bootstrapping for prediction intervals; bagging of time series models; combining forecasts; cross-validation for time series (rolling origin)
Ch. 13Practical forecasting issues — missing data imputation, outlier handling, very short series, intermittent demand (Croston's method), count data forecasting
// SOLVE
Ex. 11.1 – 11.5 (MinT reconciliation)ML LINK
Apply bottom-up, top-down, and MinT reconciliation to a 3-level hierarchy. Compare RMSE at each level. MinT reconciliation solves a constrained least-squares problem — it is a Lagrange-multiplier optimisation (Taylor Module 6, §6.5) subject to the summation constraint Sy = ỹ. Hierarchical coherence is structured prediction: outputs must satisfy global linear constraints, just as CRF decoding enforces label consistency.
Ex. 12.2 – 12.5 (forecast combination)ML LINK
Combine ETS, ARIMA, and seasonal naïve forecasts using equal weights, inverse-RMSE weights, and optimal regression weights. The combined forecast almost always beats the best individual model. Forecast combination is Bayesian model averaging — the weights are the posterior model probabilities (MacKay Module 2, Ch. 28).
Ex. 12.1 (time-series cross-validation)
Implement rolling-origin cross-validation: for each origin t₀ train on [1, t₀] and evaluate on [t₀+1, t₀+h]. Average the h-step-ahead RMSE over all origins. This is the only valid CV for time series — standard k-fold leaks future into training.
// ML CONNECTION
MinT reconciliation is optimal linear projection onto the coherence subspace — a linear algebra operation that parallels how structured prediction CRFs enforce label consistency. The reconciliation matrix is the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse of the summation constraint matrix, connecting to the constrained optimisation tools from Module 6.
[Ch. 14–15]Neural Networks & Foundation Forecasting Models WATCH ↗
// READ — FPPPY
Ch. 14Neural networks for forecasting — feed-forward NNARs, RNNs, LSTMs; the N-BEATS architecture (pure MLP with basis expansion); the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT); probabilistic outputs via quantile and distribution heads
Ch. 15Foundation forecasting models — zero-shot and few-shot forecasting; TimeGPT, Chronos, Lag-Llama; pre-training on large corpora of time series; in-context learning for forecasting

Ex. 14.1 – 14.5 (NNAR baseline)ML LINK

Foundation forecasting models are trained by the same next-token prediction objective as language models — the time series is tokenised and the model learns P(y_{t+1} | y_{1:t}) as a distribution over quantised values. Lower perplexity = better compression of the series = better forecasting (MacKay Module 2, L2).

Hamiltonian Mechanics

⚙️
Classical Mechanics

The Hamiltonian formulation of mechanics — energy as the central object, phase space as the arena — is the conceptual ancestor of several ideas now appearing at the frontier of deep learning. This module closes the loop — the physicist's toolkit becomes the ML practitioner's toolkit.
